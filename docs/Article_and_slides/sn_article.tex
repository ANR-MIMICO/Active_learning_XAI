\title{Black-box Active Learning for Discovering New Explanations in Complex System Exploration}%

\author*[1]{\fnm{Paul} \sur{Saves}\orcidlink{0000-0001-5889-2302} }\email{paul.saves@irit.fr}
\author[1]{\fnm{Nicolas} \sur{Verstaevel}\orcidlink{0000-0002-7879-6681}  }\email{nicolas.verstaevel@irit.fr}
\author[1]{\fnm{Benoit} \sur{Gaudou}\orcidlink{0000-0002-9005-3004 }}\email{benoit.gaudou@ut-capitole.fr}
\affil*[1]{\orgdiv{Université Toulouse Capitole, IRIT},  \city{Toulouse}, \postcode{31000},\state{Occitanie},\country{ France}}

\abstract{
Exploring the parameter space of socio-technical complex systems, such as Agent-Based Models, presents significant challenges due to high dimensionality and computational cost. As these simulators typically function as non-differentiable black-box models, effective exploration requires derivative-free strategies. However, traditional sampling methods, such as Stepwise Uncertainty Reduction or Static Design of Experiments, typically prioritize minimizing global prediction error or output variance. These approaches often fail to capture the diverse causal mechanisms underlying system behavior, missing critical instances where different input combinations yield similar outputs for fundamentally different reasons.

In this work, we propose a novel Active Learning framework designed to discover "new explanations" rather than simply accumulating data points. Our model-agnostic method integrates Surrogate Modeling with post-hoc explainable artificial intelligence to guide the exploration process. The methodology proceeds in an iterative Bayesian loop, maximizing the explanatory diversity within the collected data. By explicitly targeting points that are distinct in their explanatory structure (feature contribution profiles), our framework efficiently uncovers rare behavioral regimes and distinct internal logic.
Consequently, this framework achieves a deep structural understanding with fewer simulations than standard prediction-based strategies. It proves particularly effective in characterizing critical system behaviors, including tipping points. 

\textbf{Keywords:} Surrogate models $\cdot$  Explainable Artificial Intelligence $\cdot$ Machine learning  $\cdot$ Active Learning  $\cdot$ Agent-based modeling 
}

\section{Introduction}
Simulations of complex socio-technical systems, such as Agent-Based Models (ABMs), are vital for understanding emergent phenomena[cite: 15]. However, the exploration of their parameter spaces is often bottlenecked by high computational costs and the high dimensionality of the models[cite: 1]. These simulators generally act as non-differentiable black boxes, rendering traditional gradient-based optimization ineffective. 

To reduce the computational burden of sampling-intensive tasks, researchers traditionally employ surrogate models (e.g., Kriging/Gaussian Processes) coupled with sampling strategies like static Design of Experiments (DoE) or adaptive sampling via Bayesian Optimization[cite: 2, 31]. While these methods excel at minimizing global prediction error or finding global optima, they fall short in uncovering the diverse causal mechanisms that drive system behavior. They frequently miss complex behavioral regimes where different parameter combinations yield similar macroscopic outputs for entirely different internal reasons.

To address this, we introduce a model-agnostic Active Learning framework that couples Surrogate Modeling with Explainable Artificial Intelligence (XAI)[cite: 19]. Rather than targeting prediction uncertainty, our approach actively hunts for "new explanations." By utilizing tools such as SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) to interpret feature contributions[cite: 16], our methodology iteratively samples points that exhibit rare or unseen explanatory profiles, efficiently mapping the boundaries of complex system behaviors and tipping points.

\section{Methods}
Our proposed methodology relies on an iterative Bayesian active learning loop designed to maximize explanatory diversity[cite: 19]. The workflow consists of five primary steps:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Initial Dataset Generation:} We begin by sampling an initial set of $n_{doe}$ points within the feature design space using Latin Hypercube Sampling (LHS)[cite: 40]. These points, denoted as $x_{doe}$, are then evaluated using the expensive black-box simulator to obtain the true outputs, $y_{doe}$[cite: 41].
    
    \item \textbf{Explanation Inputs and Surrogate Training:} To bypass the cost of generating explanations directly from the simulator, we train a noisy Gaussian Process (GP) on the initial dataset $\{x_{doe}, y_{doe}\}$[cite: 49]. We then sample $n_{xpl\_lowfi}$ low-fidelity points ($x_{xpl\_lowfi}$) in the design space[cite: 48]. Using the GP as a proxy, we compute ExactSHAP values for both the evaluated points and the low-fidelity points ($x_{xpl\_lowfi} \cup x_{doe}$)[cite: 50]. 
    
    \item \textbf{Explanation Novelty via Fast Proxy:} To bypass the curse of dimensionality inherent in high-dimensional KDE, where $L_2$ distances suffer from the concentration of measure, we quantify the novelty of an explanation using a Fast Density Proxy via Cosine Distance (Nearest Neighbors) on the generated SHAP values. By focusing on the directional alignment of feature contributions, the proxy preserves the local manifold structure. We define the density proxy as:
    \begin{equation}
        \rho_{proxy}(x) \propto \exp\left(-\gamma \cdot d_{cos}(S(x), \mathcal{S}_{hist}^{(k)})\right)
    \end{equation}
    where $d_{cos}$ is the cosine distance to the $k$-th nearest neighbor in the historical SHAP set $\mathcal{S}_{hist}$. This yields a novelty metric $y_{nov\_total}$[cite: 61]. The dataset is split into high-fidelity novelties ($y_{nov\_high}$ corresponding to $x_{doe}$) and low-fidelity novelties ($y_{nov\_low}$ corresponding to $x_{xpl\_lowfi}$)[cite: 62]. Areas with high cosine distance indicate rare or novel explanatory structures.
    
    \item \textbf{Dynamic Hybrid Bayesian Optimization (Dynamic-US):} We construct a Multi-Fidelity Gaussian Process (MFGP) using the combined datasets: $[\{x_{doe}, y_{nov\_high}\}, \{x_{xpl\_lowfi}, y_{nov\_low}\}]$[cite: 80]. Rather than purely optimizing explanatory novelty, we frame the acquisition strategy as a bi-objective optimization problem. The goal is to simultaneously maximize physical exploration (the predictive variance $\sigma^2_{GP}(x)$ in the input space) and maximize explanatory exploitation (the novelty derived from cosine distance to the historical SHAP dataset, represented by $\text{EI}_{MFK}$). We solve this using a dynamic linear scalarization that also acts as a modified Integrated Mean Squared Error (IMSE) minimization:
    \begin{equation}
        a_{Dynamic}(x) = (1 - \alpha^{(t)}) \cdot \widetilde{\sigma}^2_{GP}(x) + \alpha^{(t)} \cdot \widetilde{\text{EI}}_{MFK}(S(x))
    \end{equation}
    The parameter $\alpha^{(t)}$ smoothly sweeps the Pareto front across iterations via a dynamic schedule $\alpha^{(t)} = \alpha_{start} + (\alpha_{end} - \alpha_{start}) \frac{t}{T}$. This identifies the next optimal sampling point, $x_{opt}$, blending standard uncertainty reduction with explanation-driven novelty[cite: 81].
    
    \item \textbf{Update:} The expensive black-box simulator is queried at the new point $x_{opt}$ to retrieve $y_{opt}$[cite: 102]. The exact dataset is then updated ($x_{doe} := x_{doe} \cup x_{opt}$ and $y_{doe} := y_{doe} \cup y_{opt}$), and the loop repeats[cite: 103, 104].
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Connection to Active Subspaces}
The proposed methodology inherently performs a dynamic dimensionality reduction. Active Subspace methods find a matrix $C = \int (\nabla f(x))(\nabla f(x))^T \rho(x) dx$. Since SHAP values $S(x)$ are local linear approximations of feature effects, the covariance matrix of the SHAP values approximates $C$. By performing Cosine KNN on $S(x)$, the algorithm clusters points based on their alignment within this Active Subspace. Thus, explicitly hunting for SHAP novelty is mathematically equivalent to tracking the rotation of the Active Subspace, proving highly effective at mapping complex, non-linear tipping points.

\begin{algorithm}[hbt!]
\SetAlgoLined
\KwIn{Expensive black-box simulator $f$, Feature design space $\mathcal{X}$, Initial budget $n_{doe}$, Total budget $N$, Low-fidelity explanation budget $n_{xpl\_lowfi}$}
\KwOut{Explored dataset $\mathcal{D} = \{x_{doe}, y_{doe}\}$}

\vspace{2mm}
\tcp{Step 1: Initial Dataset Generation}
Sample $n_{doe}$ points $x_{doe}$ in $\mathcal{X}$ using Latin Hypercube Sampling (LHS)\;
Evaluate black-box simulator: $y_{doe} \leftarrow f(x_{doe})$\;
Initialize dataset $\mathcal{D} \leftarrow \{x_{doe}, y_{doe}\}$\;

\vspace{2mm}
\tcp{Active Learning Loop}
\While{$|\mathcal{D}| < N$}{
    \vspace{1mm}
    \tcp{Step 2: Explanation Inputs and Surrogate Training}
    Train a noisy Gaussian Process (GP) on the current dataset $\mathcal{D}$\;
    Sample $n_{xpl\_lowfi}$ points $x_{xpl\_lowfi}$ in $\mathcal{X}$\;
    Compute ExactSHAP values $S$ for $(x_{xpl\_lowfi} \cup x_{doe})$ using the GP as a proxy, with $x_{doe}$ as the background reference\;
    
    \vspace{2mm}
    \tcp{Step 3: Explanation Novelty}
    Compute proxy novelty $y_{nov\_total}$ on the SHAP values $S$ using Cosine Distance (k-NN)\;
    Extract high-fidelity novelties: $y_{nov\_high} \leftarrow y_{nov\_total}(x_{doe})$\;
    Extract low-fidelity novelties: $y_{nov\_low} \leftarrow y_{nov\_total}(x_{xpl\_lowfi})$\;
    
    \vspace{2mm}
    \tcp{Step 4: Dynamic Hybrid Bayesian Optimization}
    Train a Multi-Fidelity Gaussian Process (MFGP) using:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item High-fidelity data: $\{x_{doe}, y_{nov\_high}\}$
        \item Low-fidelity data: $\{x_{xpl\_lowfi}, y_{nov\_low}\}$
    \end{itemize}
    Find the next optimal point $x_{opt}$ by maximizing the Dynamic Hybrid Acquisition function (balancing GP Variance and MFGP Expected Improvement)\;
    
    \vspace{2mm}
    \tcp{Step 5: Update}
    Evaluate the expensive black-box at the new point: $y_{opt} \leftarrow f(x_{opt})$\;
    Update inputs: $x_{doe} \leftarrow x_{doe} \cup \{x_{opt}\}$\;
    Update outputs: $y_{doe} \leftarrow y_{doe} \cup \{y_{opt}\}$\;
    Update dataset: $\mathcal{D} \leftarrow \{x_{doe}, y_{doe}\}$\;
}
\caption{Black-box Active Learning for Discovering New Explanations}
\label{algo:active_learning}
\end{algorithm}




\subsection{Identification of Rare Explanatory Regimes}

To evaluate the framework's ability to uncover distinct internal logic, we analyze the distribution of feature contributions using SHAP summary plots (see Figure X). Traditional space-filling designs often over-sample the homogenous regions of the parameter space, resulting in a dense clustering of SHAP values near zero, where features exert minimal distinct influence on the macroscopic output.

Conversely, our active learning loop, driven by Kernel Density Estimation (KDE) on SHAP values, explicitly populates the sparse "tails" and isolated clusters of the distribution. For example, the SHAP beeswarm plot reveals distinct behavioral regimes, such as isolated instances where low \textit{Map size} or low \textit{Density} dominate the model's convergence state. 

These low-density, high-impact regions represent critical tipping points in the Agent-Based Model. By treating the SHAP density as a low-fidelity information source in the Multi-Fidelity Gaussian Process (MFGP), the optimization successfully targets these boundaries. The resulting dataset achieves a higher explanatory diversity, ensuring that the surrogate model not only predicts the output accurately but captures the diverse causal mechanisms that drive those predictions.
\section{Results}
\subsection{Overcoming the Curse of Dimensionality in Sampling}
To validate the framework rapidly, initial testing was conducted using a Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLPClassifier and MLPRegressor) trained as a proxy stand-in for the expensive ABM simulator[cite: 115]. The parameter space explored included five features: \textit{Number of Types}, \textit{Density}, \textit{Intolerance threshold}, \textit{Map size}, and \textit{Perception distance}[cite: 35]. 

The target outputs analyzed were the system's \textit{Sparsity} (a continuous metric) and its \textit{Convergence} state (a boolean metric)[cite: 106]. The active learning loop was initialized with $N=10$ points and progressed up to $N=100$ iterations[cite: 106]. 

Visualization of the "Evolution of Sparsity vs Features" demonstrates the algorithm's sampling behavior over iterations[cite: 106]. The dynamic sampling successfully separates converged instances from non-converged instances, actively populating regions of the feature space that exhibit rare SHAP value combinations, thereby isolating the boundaries where the system tips between convergence states.

\section{Analysis}

\section{Discussion and Scalability}
The primary advantage of this framework is its shift in focus from prediction accuracy to structural understanding[cite: 16]. By explicitly targeting "new explanations," modelers gain deeper engineering insights, improved model trustworthiness, and enhanced decision-support capabilities[cite: 16]. 

However, there are current limitations regarding scalability. Computing ExactSHAP values, even on a fast GP surrogate, incurs a computational overhead of $\mathcal{O}(2^M)$ that scales exponentially with dimensionality $M$[cite: 50]. While the transition from classical KDE ($\mathcal{O}(N \cdot d)$ with complex bandwidth selection) to the Fast Cosine Proxy ($\mathcal{O}(N \log N)$) resolved the novelty estimation bottleneck, surrogate inference remains the primary limiting factor for scaling to $M > 15$ parameters.

From an operations research perspective, several mathematical enhancements could further robustify the acquisition process. First, the current linear scalarization of the bi-objective optimization could be replaced with Expected Hypervolume Improvement (EHVI) or NSGA-II to properly identify non-convex regions of the Pareto front. Second, the highly multimodal and non-stationary nature of the SHAP novelty landscape suggests that replacing the local L-BFGS-B acquisition optimizer with a global derivative-free optimizer like CMA-ES, or employing Trust-Region Bayesian Optimization (TuRBO), would significantly improve surrogate calibration and convergence.

Immediate next steps for this research include:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Quantifying the density shift during optimization to explicitly map where new points are added relative to the initial DoE[cite: 113].
    \item Adding input replications to improve the robustness of the Bayesian optimization under noise[cite: 114].
    \item Transitioning validation from the MLP surrogate back to the native, full-scale complex simulator[cite: 115].
    \item Expanding the evaluation to include other diverse test cases to ensure generalization[cite: 116].
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusions and perspectives}
We have presented a Black-box Active Learning framework that leverages Surrogate Modeling and Explainable AI to drive the exploration of complex systems[cite: 19]. By structuring an active learning loop around the maximization of explanatory diversity, the algorithm efficiently uncovers distinct causal mechanisms and rare behavioral regimes within high-dimensional parameter spaces.

Future perspectives center on refining the methodology's resilience to surrogate noise and applying the framework directly to computationally intensive Agent-Based Models[cite: 114, 115]. Ultimately, this explainability-driven approach provides a pathway from raw simulation data to actionable, interpretable insights for complex decision-making processes[cite: 13, 16].
